Filter is sector-specific. For oil, operating-profit-up captures business performance better than revenue-up because revenue moves with crude oil prices.

Why these filter options?

Why use Operating profit ↑ (default for Oil)?
In plain terms: Oil sector revenue moves with global crude oil prices — NOT with how well the business is being run. When oil prices fall, every refiner's revenue drops, even if they're running their refineries efficiently and earning healthy refining margins. So 'revenue up (売上○)' is a misleading quality signal for oil companies — it mostly tells you about OPEC decisions and global demand, not about the company itself.



Operating profit (営業利益) captures the actual business performance: refining margins, inventory management, cost discipline, downstream demand strength. When operating profit grows, the business is genuinely doing better, regardless of whether crude prices were a tailwind or a headwind.



That's why for the oil sector, this view defaults to 営業利益○ (operating profit up). For FY3/2026, crude oil prices fell sharply, so almost every Japanese refiner saw revenue decline (-4% to -12%). But operating profit grew strongly at the top players (Idemitsu +31%, ENEOS +340%, Cosmo +13%) because refining margins were favorable. The strict 売上○ filter would exclude all of these stories — which is why the profit-based filter shows the real picture.



You can switch to 売上○ if you want only revenue-growth companies (this year that's just Fuji Kosan, thanks to diversification into environmental energy and recycling). Or 両方 (both) to see all companies tagged under either filter.
Why use Revenue ↑ (strict)?
In plain terms: This is the strict 'revenue up' filter — same as the IT sector default. For oil companies, this filter usually excludes most of the universe because crude oil price declines drag down revenue across the sector even when business is performing well. Use this if your senior or framework specifically requires a revenue-based filter; otherwise the 営業利益○ default is more informative for the sector.
Why use Either ↑ (revenue OR operating profit up)?
In plain terms: This is the union of the two filters above. Companies that have EITHER revenue up OR operating profit up appear. Use this when you want the broadest view of the sector and don't want to be restricted to one signal. For oil this typically shows the same set as 営業利益○ since the revenue-up subset is a small intersection of the OP-up set.

What happened: Idemitsu Kosan is Japan's #2 oil refiner and distributor — about 30% domestic gasoline station share, with refineries in Hokkaido, Chiba, Aichi, and Tokuyama. Plus a 'High-Functional Materials' segment (organic-EL blue emitter materials, lithium-ion battery materials, overseas lubricant sales) gives Idemitsu a unique diversification axis. FY3/2026 revenue ¥8.106 trillion (-11.8%), operating profit ¥212.2 billion (+30.8%), net profit ¥171.9 billion (+65.2%). Revenue down but profit sharply up. Why profit grew: First, positive inventory time-lag effect in fuel oil business — crude price spikes lifted inventory valuation. Second, improved refinery utilization — large maintenance turnarounds were less impactful than the previous year. Third, strong High-Functional Materials segment — overseas lubricant sales grew, and organic-EL material demand (for Korea and China) continued. Stock reaction: Stock fell from ¥1,519 in March 2026 to ¥1,357 in June 2026 — a -10.6% drop over 3 months. Earnings are strong but the stock moved the opposite way — the classic '売上○株価×' pattern (or '営業利益○株価×' on a profit basis). Why is the stock falling despite strong earnings — structural reasons: First, weakness in FY27 guidance. Idemitsu guided FY27 net profit at ¥7.5 billion (-56.4% YoY) — falling crude prices will produce a negative time-lag effect. The market is pricing the 'good year now, payback next year' scenario, and forward concerns weigh on the current price even if it looks cheap. Second, structural risk from crude price dependency. Most of the profit comes from inventory valuation effects (= change in crude price), making the underlying business performance hard to see. Institutional investors judge that 'temporary profit doesn't deserve a higher multiple.' Third, cost of decarbonization regulation. Oil refining must shrink long-term — even though Idemitsu's High-Functional Materials business has strong EU share, the core revenue is still refining, and energy-transition uncertainty weighs on valuation. Fourth, PBR-improvement measures in progress but with limited market reward. Idemitsu has executed PBR-improvement measures (buybacks, dividend hikes, stock splits, mid-term plan revisions) and the stock did rally when measures were announced, but it's now at a 'will the measures keep working?' stage. PBR improved from 0.55x pre-announcement by 0.29 points, but is still depressed. Fifth, ESG screens at institutional investors. ESG-focused investors limit fossil-fuel exposure — even with earnings improvement, the ESG-screen wall keeps many investors from adding. Potential re-rating catalysts: - Mid-term plan targets FY30 ROE 13%, profit +50% vs current - High-Functional Materials business (organic-EL blue, solid-state battery, renewables, advanced materials) is now positioned as the growth driver - 20-year shareholder equity expanded 3.2x — strong commitment to shareholder returns Risks: (a) FY27 negative inventory effect compressing profit, (b) long-term structural decline in refining, (c) competition from Korean / Chinese makers in materials, (d) Middle East tensions affecting crude procurement.

What happened: ENEOS Holdings (renamed from JXTG Energy) is Japan's largest oil refiner and distributor — about 50% market share in domestic gasoline stations, industry-leading refinery utilization. Subsidiaries include ENEOS (refining/distribution), JX Metals (copper / electronic materials), and JX Oil & Gas Exploration (upstream E&P) — an integrated energy company. FY3/2026 revenue ¥11.77 trillion (-4.5%), operating profit ¥466.6 billion (+340%), net profit ¥258.7 billion (EPS ¥96.18) — revenue declined on falling crude oil prices, but operating profit surged over 4x. Why operating profit grew: The largest factor was that inventory valuation losses came in far smaller than expected. Against an initial guidance assuming inventory writedown impact of ¥130 billion, the actual impact was just ¥7.8 billion. This was because the March 2026 Middle East tensions caused crude prices to spike, supporting inventory valuations more than anticipated. Refining margins (the spread between product prices and crude prices) also improved across the industry, lifting profit further. Stock reaction: Stock outperformed the Nikkei 225 by +8.7% over the past 6 months — institutional buyers stepped in on the EPS beat vs. guidance, dividend hike, and strong FY27 guidance (revenue +9.2% to ¥12.85 trillion, OP +31% to ¥610 billion, EPS ¥154.28). Structural drivers of the re-rating: First, stable cash-flow generation from refining. Japan's gasoline demand is in gradual decline, but industry consolidation (absorbing Toa Oil, the Fuji Oil TOB) has raised oligopoly concentration — ENEOS is now seen as a 'reliably profitable refining business.' Second, clear energy-transition strategy. ENEOS has set explicit 2025-2030 targets to shift toward renewables, hydrogen, and CCS (carbon capture and storage) with a clearly stated decarbonization roadmap. This brought ESG investors into the buyer base. Third, strengthened dividend and buyback policy. FY26 dividend hike announced + continued buybacks. High dividend yield (around 4-5%) and PBR around 1.0x have attracted income-oriented investors. Fourth, upstream E&P subsidiary profit contribution. JX Oil & Gas Exploration delivers profit from overseas oil/gas concession interests; JX Metals' copper / electronic-materials business (driven by EV demand) provides earnings beyond refining. Fifth, institutional Buy ratings. Major brokerages rate ENEOS Buy overall, with target prices above current levels. The market expects the persistent PBR ~0.7x discount to close. Risks: (a) inventory valuation swings from crude price volatility, (b) long-term decline in domestic gasoline demand, (c) structural pressure on the oil business from tighter decarbonization rules, (d) delayed monetization of new businesses (renewables, hydrogen).

Revenue down, but profit metrics improved across the board — net profit up 28%. Second, the renewable energy business turned profitable — the wind-power-led renewable segment achieved first-ever profitability, validating the diversification story. Recent earnings improvement plus stronger shareholder returns have lifted institutional ratings. Structural drivers of the re-rating: First, Hail oilfield upstream revenue. Hail concession crude production absorbs FX and crude-price volatility while generating stable cash flow. The integrated upstream-to-downstream model improves earnings predictability. Second, renewable energy turning profitable as an independent earnings source. Cosmo Eco Power's wind business achieving profitability signals that an oil company's decarbonization shift is becoming real earnings, not just an ESG PR exercise. Institutional investors are starting to value this as a long-term growth axis. Third, aggressive share buybacks. Cosmo announced a buyback of up to ¥25 billion, capped at 6.25 million shares (3.81% of outstanding) — clearly signaling intent to lift EPS and ROE and close the PBR discount. Fourth, equity ratio improving. Equity ratio rose to 27.6% — at or above industry average and improving — easing concerns about financial health. Fifth, analyst ratings improving. The 7-analyst average rating of 2.13 (1 = strong buy, 5 = strong sell) leans heavily Buy. The average target of ¥4,983 implies meaningful upside. Risks: (a) Middle East geopolitical risk affecting Hail production and shipping, (b) sudden refining margin compression, (c) wind maintenance / weather risks, (d) FY27 guidance shows a profit DECLINE due to Middle East situation concerns — near-term caution warranted.

While ENEOS, Idemitsu, Cosmo are swung by crude prices and inventory effects, Fuji Kosan's 'environmental energy + recycling' theme is a unique ESG story — an attractive option for investors looking beyond pure commodity exposure.
